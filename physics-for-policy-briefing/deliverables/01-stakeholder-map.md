# Deliverable 1 — Stakeholder Map

**Policy question:** How should commercial nuclear fusion energy facilities be
licensed and regulated?

This map identifies the actors whose interests, powers, and positions shape the
fusion regulatory debate. Stakeholders are grouped into four categories —
**regulators, industry, researchers, and public-interest groups** — as required
by the brief. Each entry records the actor's interest, formal power, stated or
inferred position, and the evidence source.

Positions are coded:

- **PRO-PROPORTIONATE** — favours a light, fusion-specific regime distinct from fission licensing.
- **PRO-PRECAUTIONARY** — favours stronger or fission-equivalent oversight.
- **NEUTRAL / PROCESS** — primarily concerned with clarity, consistency, or process integrity.

## Power / interest summary

- **High power, high interest (manage closely):** NRC, UK DESNZ + EA/HSE, IAEA, private developers.
- **High power, lower interest (keep satisfied):** US Congress, ONR, fission industry.
- **Lower power, high interest (keep informed):** environmental groups, host communities, safety researchers, non-proliferation community.

## Key fault line

The central tension is **proportionate vs. precautionary** regulation. The
dominant institutional position (NRC, UK Government, Agile Nations) is
pro-proportionate: fusion lacks fission's runaway-reaction and long-lived
high-level-waste hazards, so a byproduct-material / non-site-licence regime is
appropriate [nrc2023vote], [ukfusionregfactsheet]. The counter-pressure comes
from environmental and non-proliferation interests worried that tritium releases
and large volumes of neutron-activated material are being under-weighted
[iaeatritiumharmonization], [radwasterecycling2023]. The policy options in
Deliverable 4 are designed around this fault line.
