## Theme 1 — Fusion's hazard profile is fundamentally different from fission

**Finding (high confidence).** The dominant hazards of a fusion facility are (a)
**tritium**, a mildly radioactive hydrogen isotope used as fuel, and (b)
**neutron-activated materials** — reactor components made radioactive by the
intense neutron flux. There is **no fissile material, no self-sustaining chain
reaction, and no risk of a meltdown or runaway reaction** [ukfusionregfactsheet],
[iterstafetybasis]. The fusion reaction self-terminates within seconds if plasma
conditions are not maintained, a point the IAEA characterises as making the
process inherently safe [ukfusionregfactsheet].

This is the load-bearing scientific claim behind every proportionate-regulation
argument. It is strongly supported: the ITER safety basis explicitly derives
from these fundamental characteristics, with radiological hazards limited to
tritium and activated material [iterstafetybasis].

**Caveat.** "Inherently safer than fission" is **not** "risk-free." Peer-reviewed
analysis stresses that operating plants still face real hazards across
construction, operation, and decommissioning that require active management
[proportionate2024].

## Theme 2 — Tritium is the defining radiological control problem

**Finding (high confidence).** Tritium's radiotoxicity is low and most ingested
or inhaled tritium leaves the body before decaying, but it must still be handled
with releases tightly limited [ukfusionregfactsheet], [tritiumbreeding2018]. A
commercial deuterium-tritium plant must also *breed* its own tritium in a lithium
blanket, creating a closed fuel cycle whose accounting and containment dominate
safety design [iaeatecdoc2049], [tritiumbreeding2018].

**Why it matters for regulation.** Accidental atmospheric tritium release is the
modelled worst-case consequence at fusion facilities, which is why the IAEA is
actively harmonising **models for accidental tritium releases** across countries
[iaeatritiumharmonization]. Any proportionate regime still needs credible tritium
discharge limits, monitoring, and emergency planning.

## Theme 3 — Activated materials and waste: smaller and shorter-lived, but not zero

**Finding (medium-high confidence).** Fusion does not produce long-lived,
high-level radioactive waste of the kind fission spent fuel represents
[ukfusionregfactsheet]. Most fusion waste is **low-level waste** arising from
periodic replacement of in-vessel components activated by neutrons
[radwasterecycling2023]. Comparative technical analysis finds fusion waste is
less hazardous than fast-fission waste, and substantially so if advanced
structural materials (e.g. silicon carbide or vanadium alloys) are used.

**Disagreement / open question.** Researchers argue fusion has historically
defaulted to fission-style **land-based disposal**, and that **recycling and
clearance** strategies would better fit fusion's activation profile
[radwasterecycling2023]. There is also a methodological warning: fission
irradiation data cannot be reliably scaled to predict fusion material behaviour,
so activation and waste volumes carry genuine modelling uncertainty
[tritiumbreeding2018].

## Theme 4 — The US chose the "Part 30 byproduct material" route

**Finding (high confidence, official).** The NRC considered three options and in
April 2023 voted for the **byproduct-material framework under 10 CFR Part 30** —
the same regime used for particle accelerators and similar technologies — rather
than the reactor licensing framework used for fission [nrc2023vote],
[nrc2023frnotice], [cfr10part30]. In February 2026 the NRC published a **proposed
rule, "Regulatory Framework for Fusion Machines,"** that amends the
byproduct-material framework to cover fusion, focusing on the radioactive
materials (tritium, activation products) rather than on the machine as a complex
engineered reactor [nrc2026proposedrule], [orrick2026].

**Significance.** This is a limited-scope rulemaking, deliberately lighter and
faster than reactor licensing, and is the single most consequential US regulatory
decision for near-term commercialisation [orrick2026].

## Theme 5 — The UK reached the same destination by a different legal route

**Finding (high confidence, official).** The UK amended the **Nuclear
Installations Act 1965** to confirm that fusion facilities are **not** subject to
nuclear site licensing, on the explicit ground that such licensing — designed for
higher-hazard fissile-material sites — would be **disproportionate** for fusion
[ukfusionregfactsheet]. UK fusion is therefore regulated by the **Environment
Agency and the Health and Safety Executive**, not the Office for Nuclear
Regulation [ukenvagency2022]. The UK ties this to an industrial goal: a prototype
plant (STEP) at West Burton by 2040 and an exportable fusion industry
[uktowardsfusion2023]. Siting is being addressed through a draft National Policy
Statement (EN-8) [uken8nps2026].

**Convergence.** The US (statutory byproduct framework) and UK (statutory
exclusion from site licensing) differ in legal mechanism but converge on the same
principle: **regulate fusion in proportion to its hazard, separately from
fission** [nrc2023vote], [ukfusionregfactsheet], [agilenations2023].

## Theme 6 — International harmonisation is early but active

**Finding (medium confidence).** The IAEA hosted its **first technical meeting on
fusion safety and regulation in November 2023** and is building shared safety
knowledge and tritium-release modelling [iaea2023meeting], [iaeatritiumharmonization].
The Agile Nations group (UK, Japan, Canada) issued joint recommendations favouring
hazard-based frameworks [agilenations2023]. Harmonisation matters because fusion
developers operate globally and fragmented national rules raise compliance cost
and the risk of regulatory arbitrage.

**Gap.** Unlike fission, fusion has **no mature international safety-standards
regime** yet; the IAEA effort is nascent and non-binding [iaeafusionsafety].

## Theme 7 — Commercialisation is plausibly near-term, which raises the stakes

**Finding (medium confidence).** Private fusion firms target grid connection in
the **early 2030s**; global private investment exceeds several billion dollars
(the UK cites ~$5bn to date; later surveys put cumulative figures higher)
[ukfusionregfactsheet], [crs2026fusion]. The CRS cautions that significant
scientific and engineering hurdles to commercial viability remain
[crs2026fusion]. Methodologists note that designing safety frameworks for
first-of-a-kind systems with **limited operational experience** is itself a
challenge, and propose technology-agnostic Safety-in-Design approaches
[sidmethodology2026].

**Implication.** Regulators face a timing problem: act early enough to give
developers certainty, but avoid locking in a framework before activation and
tritium data mature.

## What is well established vs. contested vs. unknown

| Status | Claim |
|--------|-------|
| **Well established** | No chain reaction / meltdown risk; no long-lived high-level waste; tritium and activation are the main hazards [iterstafetybasis], [ukfusionregfactsheet]. |
| **Well established** | US (Part 30) and UK (no site licence) have both chosen proportionate, fission-distinct regimes [nrc2023vote], [ukfusionregfactsheet]. |
| **Contested** | Whether fusion waste should default to disposal or move to recycling/clearance [radwasterecycling2023]. |
| **Contested** | Whether a light regime adequately controls tritium discharge and large activated-material volumes [iaeatritiumharmonization]. |
| **Uncertain / unknown** | Real-world activation inventories and waste volumes (fission data does not scale reliably) [tritiumbreeding2018]. |
| **Uncertain / unknown** | Commercial viability date and which confinement approach reaches grid first [crs2026fusion]. |
